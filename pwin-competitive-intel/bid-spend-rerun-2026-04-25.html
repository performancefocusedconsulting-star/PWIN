Bid Spend Re-Run · 25 April 2026

The picture, with the new data

In April we estimated UK government strategic suppliers spend about £3.9 billion a year pursuing contracts. That figure was built on Find a Tender data alone, and the original analysis flagged a known gap: the older feed only captures about 15% of small contracts, so the smallest tier had to be estimated bottom-up.

Today the database also holds five years of Contracts Finder data — the feed that captures small and sub-threshold contracts. This page re-runs the original methodology against the combined dataset and shows how much, if anything, the answer has moved.

Window: trailing 12 months to 25 April 2026 · 37 strategic supplier groups, 95 name patterns · methodology unchanged from 23 April 2026 CFO model

Headline numbers

Three runs of the same model, against three datasets. The first replays the original baseline; the second re-runs it on today's database (older feed only); the third uses both feeds combined.

£3.93bn

Re-run today · older feed only
£3.57bn

Re-run today · combined data
£4.96bn

10,651 bids/year. Same method, both feeds. +39% on the older-feed-only figure. Sunk cost on losing bids: £3.3bn (was £2bn).

What this confirms

The biggest gains are in the smaller tiers — exactly where the original analysis warned the older feed was blind. Contracts Finder reveals 776 framework call-offs won by strategic suppliers that the older feed missed entirely (165 → 941 visible wins, a five-fold increase).

The big-contract tiers (strategic programme, defence mega, major services) move much less. This makes sense: those contracts are above legal publication threshold and the older feed already captured them. Adding Contracts Finder mostly fills in below-threshold work.

What this means for the commercial story

The picture is stronger, not weaker

You can keep using the conservative £3bn pursuit / £2bn wasted headline numbers — they remain defensible. Or you can lead with the larger figures the better dataset supports: £5bn pursuit / £3.3bn wasted.

Either way, the case has not collapsed. New data has confirmed and slightly extended the original finding.

The methodology now stands up better

The original analysis admitted it had to estimate the small-contract tier because the older feed couldn't see most of it. That estimate is no longer a guess — Contracts Finder lets us count the small contracts directly, and the original 8,000-bid estimate looks reasonable.

If a CFO challenges the small-contract assumption, you now have evidence rather than rhetoric.

The strategic-supplier population is wider than visible

Strategic-supplier-won competitions over the year went from 339 visible in the older feed to 1,295 visible with both feeds — nearly 4× as many wins now appear in the data.

This matters when telling individual supplier stories. Capita, Mitie, Serco's full activity is much more visible.

BidEquity's pursuit-elimination value scales with the new headline

20% pursuit elimination, applied to the combined £4.96bn industry spend, saves ~£990m industry-wide — up from ~£715m on the older-feed-only number.

Per strategic supplier on average: ~£27m/year recoverable, up from ~£19m. The retainer ROI argument gets stronger automatically.

How this was generated

Reproducible re-run. The same script will produce the same numbers tomorrow if you run it again — assuming no major data load in between.

  • Database: pwin-competitive-intel/db/bid_intel.db as of 25 April 2026
  • Script: pwin-competitive-intel/queries/bid_spend_recalc.py — re-runnable, prints to terminal
  • Window: trailing 12 months from today (2025-04-25 → 2026-04-25)
  • Strategic supplier list: 37 Cabinet Office groups, matched via 95 name patterns against the supplier table
  • Bid-cost figures: unchanged from the 23 April 2026 CFO model — calibrated against real disclosed bid costs (Selborne £1.2bn / 2yr / £12m; framework call-offs at £35k empirical)
  • Win rate assumption: 33% baseline (unchanged from original)
