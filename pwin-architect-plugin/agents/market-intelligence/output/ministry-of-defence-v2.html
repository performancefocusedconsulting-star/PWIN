The Ministry of Defence is the UK's largest government department by budget, responsible for all elements of national defence including the Armed Forces, equipment procurement, intelligence, and nuclear deterrent. It operates through a complex structure of military commands, executive agencies (DE&S, Dstl, DIO, SDA), and arm's length bodies. The SDR 2025 has triggered the most significant defence transformation programme in a generation, with a new Military Strategic Headquarters, restructured commands, and a commitment to reach 2.5% of GDP on defence by 2034. Procurement reform is a central theme — the department aspires to reduce contract award timelines from years to a two-year maximum. For suppliers, this is a buyer undergoing fundamental structural change while simultaneously increasing its spending by over £10 billion over the Spending Review period.

Strategic Priorities & External Pressures
high
The MOD is in the midst of its most significant strategic reset since the Cold War. The Strategic Defence Review 2025 sets a ten-year vision centred on warfighting readiness, integration of AI and autonomy into conventional forces, and a fundamental restructuring of how defence is organised and delivered. The Defence Industrial Strategy 2025 complements this with a commitment to reform procurement, innovate at 'wartime pace', and grow the UK defence industrial base. The ambition is vast but the implementation gap is already being flagged — FY2025-26 is a transitional year with quarterly reform milestones, and the bulk of transformation is due by FY2026-27.
Regulatory Pressures
Procurement Act 2023 (enacted February 2024) introduces new rules for defence procurement, including provisions for defence-specific exemptions. AUKUS treaty obligations shape submarine and technology procurement partnerships.
fact high [SRC-009]
Published legislation and treaty commitments
Political Pressures
Strong cross-party support for increased defence spending following Ukraine conflict and deteriorating global security environment. SDR was a Labour manifesto commitment. Political pressure to demonstrate value from the additional spending.

Procurement Behaviour
high
MOD is the UK's largest public sector buyer with 349 awards totalling £47.3 billion in the FTS dataset. Procurement is overwhelmingly through limited (single-source or negotiated) and selective (restricted) procedures — open competition accounts for only 3% of awards. The portfolio is heavily weighted towards goods and supplies (particularly fuel and equipment) with significant services spend across BPO, technology, construction, and professional services. Framework agreements are used extensively, particularly through CCS and MOD-specific frameworks. The buyer entity fragments across multiple procurement organisations (DE&S, Dstl, DIO, SDA) each with their own processes and supplier relationships.

Supplier Ecosystem
medium
The MOD supplier ecosystem is dominated by a small number of large defence primes (BAE Systems, Babcock, Rolls-Royce, Serco, Capita) who hold the strategic relationships and major long-term contracts. The fuel supply chain (BP, Agility, World Fuels) accounts for the largest single contract values. Below the primes, a broad SME ecosystem supplies through framework call-offs and subcontracting arrangements. The SDR and Defence Industrial Strategy signal an intention to broaden the supplier base, particularly for technology and innovation, but procurement data still shows overwhelming concentration among established suppliers with 77% of awards through single-source procedures.
