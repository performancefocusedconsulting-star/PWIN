# Action Playbooks

Select the appropriate playbook based on the scenario classification and scoring results.
Multiple playbooks may apply — for example, a challenger displacement with high TUPE risk
should apply both the challenger displacement playbook and the high TUPE risk playbook.

## 1. Challenger Displacement

Use when the bidder is not the incumbent and is seeking to displace a known external supplier.

### Positioning Principles
- Respect the current service foundation — never imply the buyer was wrong to choose the incumbent
- Position the next phase as evolution, not replacement
- Make switching feel controlled, low-risk, and evidence-based
- Show how continuity will be protected while improvement is introduced
- Translate every identified incumbent weakness into a forward-looking benefit
- Lead with your positive proposition, not the incumbent's shortcomings

### Required Proof Points
- Comparable transition case studies (same sector, similar scale, similar complexity)
- Mobilisation plan with named governance and milestones
- Knowledge transfer method and timeline
- TUPE and workforce approach (where applicable)
- Data migration and systems integration approach (where applicable)
- Service continuity and hypercare model
- Governance and risk controls during transition
- Early-life performance commitments and remedies

### Messages to Use
- "The next phase of service maturity"
- "Continuity with targeted improvement"
- "Controlled transition"
- "Protect what works and improve what matters"
- "Evidence-led mobilisation"
- "Lower-risk transformation"
- "Outcome-focused evolution"
- "Fresh perspective with proven mobilisation discipline"
- "Building on the existing foundation"

### Messages to Avoid
- "Incumbent failure"
- "Broken service"
- "Replacing poor delivery"
- "Wholesale reset"
- "They have not delivered"
- "The current provider is weak"
- "Complete overhaul"
- "Starting from scratch"
- Any language that implies the buyer made a poor previous decision

## 2. Defensive Re-compete

Use when the bidder is the current incumbent seeking to retain the contract.

### Positioning Principles
- Combine continuity with credible improvement — continuity alone is not a winning position
- Avoid complacent reliance on historical relationship
- Show measurable value already delivered, with specifics
- Acknowledge lessons learned where tactically appropriate — evaluators know your weaknesses
- Demonstrate investment, innovation, and next-phase roadmap
- Neutralise known weaknesses before competitors exploit them
- Show you are competing for the work, not assuming it

### Required Proof Points
- Performance track record with quantified outcomes
- Benefits delivered (financial, operational, user satisfaction)
- User satisfaction improvements over the contract period
- Continuous improvement roadmap with specific commitments
- Corrective actions for known issues (with evidence they worked)
- Innovation commitments for the next contract period
- Governance and transparency improvements
- Investment already made in the service (people, systems, processes)

### Messages to Use
- "Proven continuity with renewed ambition"
- "Lessons learned and embedded improvement"
- "Lower-risk next phase"
- "Established knowledge with fresh investment"
- "Measurable service maturity"
- "We are competing for this, not assuming it"
- "Built on [X years] of operational insight"

### Messages to Avoid
- "We know you best"
- "No transition risk"
- "Business as usual"
- "Continuation of current model"
- "Trust us because we are already here"
- "Why change what works" (implies no need for improvement)
- Any language that sounds entitled or complacent

## 3. High TUPE Risk

Apply alongside the primary playbook whenever TUPE and workforce transfer are material
factors. This is common in managed services, facilities management, contact centres,
field services, operational delivery, and outsourced support functions.

### Actions
- Escalate to HR, legal, commercial, and delivery leads before final go decision
- Confirm whether TUPE applies and what workforce data is available from the buyer or outgoing provider
- Model cost implications: liabilities, pensions, terms and conditions harmonisation, absence rates, productivity assumptions, and industrial relations risks
- Include a workforce engagement approach — how will transferring staff be communicated with, supported, and integrated
- Build a transition communications plan for all affected stakeholders
- Avoid promising savings that are incompatible with workforce transfer cost assumptions — experienced evaluators will test this
- Assess whether the buyer has provided (or will provide) TUPE information in the procurement — and what to do if they have not

### Commercial Implications
- TUPE cost absorption must be reflected in the pricing model
- Do not assume productivity improvements from transferring staff in year one
- Pension risk (defined benefit vs. defined contribution) can be a go/no-go factor on its own
- Industrial relations risk (recognised unions, existing agreements) must be assessed

## 4. High Systems and Data Dependency

Apply alongside the primary playbook whenever the incumbent controls systems, data, or
interfaces that the service depends on.

### Actions
- Identify incumbent-controlled systems, data stores, interfaces, documentation, and reporting processes
- Assess exit obligations in the current contract — are data transfer provisions adequate?
- Build a data migration and systems access plan with timeline
- Include an early discovery and transition validation phase in the mobilisation plan
- Ask clarification questions on data quality, system ownership, integration constraints, and documentation availability
- Assess whether the buyer owns or licences the core systems (buyer-owned systems reduce incumbent stickiness)
- Identify any proprietary tools or platforms the incumbent has introduced that the buyer now depends on

### Risk Factors
- Undocumented interfaces and workarounds are common — assume they exist
- Data quality in legacy systems is often poor — build data cleansing time into the transition plan
- System access may be withdrawn on contract end — plan for parallel running
- Intellectual property in bespoke tools may belong to the incumbent, not the buyer

## 5. Buyer Low Change Appetite

Apply when the buyer change appetite score is 1–2 and the primary recommendation is
no-go, selective-go, or go-with-conditions.

### Actions
- Avoid radical transformation positioning — it will increase buyer anxiety
- Emphasise continuity, assurance, incremental value, and low disruption
- Test appetite for change through clarification questions or pre-market engagement
- Identify whether price, resilience, compliance, or innovation is the true buyer driver — it may not be change
- Consider no-go if challenger differentiation cannot overcome inertia
- Look for specific pain points the buyer cannot ignore (regulatory requirement, audit finding, ministerial mandate) that may force change despite low general appetite

### Positioning Adjustment
- Frame your offer as "the safest path to improvement" rather than "transformation"
- Lead with risk reduction and service assurance, not innovation
- Show that your approach minimises change for the buyer's operational teams
- Provide a phased implementation that allows the buyer to control the pace

## 6. Unknown Incumbent

Apply when the incumbent identity is unknown or unclear and the scenario is classified
as `unknown_incumbent`.

### Actions
- Flag inability to complete a full assessment — state this clearly in the executive judgement
- Generate intelligence requirements: what do we need to know, and where might we find it
- Search contract history, award notices (Contracts Finder, Find a Tender, Tussell), buyer reports, and procurement documents for incumbent identity clues
- Ask the capture/sales team to validate the current delivery model
- Produce provisional scenarios: "If the incumbent is a large prime, then X. If in-house, then Y."
- Assess whether the buyer has deliberately withheld incumbent identity (unusual but possible) and what that might signal

### Intelligence Sources to Check
- Contracts Finder / Find a Tender award notices
- Tussell contract data
- Buyer annual reports and accounts
- FOI requests (if time permits)
- LinkedIn (incumbent staff listing the buyer as a client)
- Supplier press releases and case studies
- Parliamentary questions mentioning the service

## 7. Framework-Constrained Procurement

Apply when the procurement is a call-off from an existing framework (CCS, Crown Commercial
Service, DPS, bespoke framework) and framework rules affect competitive dynamics.

### Actions
- Confirm which framework the call-off uses and whether the bidder is on it
- Assess whether framework lot structure constrains who can bid for which scope
- Identify whether the incumbent has a preferred or established position on the framework
- Check framework expiry date — approaching expiry may signal a re-procurement window
- Assess whether framework terms (e.g., ceiling rates, standard Ts&Cs) constrain commercial differentiation
- Evaluate whether the framework evaluation model differs from open procurement (often simpler, faster, more price-sensitive)

### Strategic Considerations
- Framework call-offs often have shorter timescales — bid investment must be proportionate
- Incumbent advantage may be amplified on frameworks where the buyer has used them before
- Some frameworks require mini-competition; others allow direct award — the competitive dynamic is fundamentally different
- Framework pricing transparency may reveal incumbent pricing, creating an intelligence advantage

## 8. SME Challenger

Apply when the bidding organisation is an SME competing against a larger incumbent or
alongside larger challengers.

### Actions
- Assess whether PQQ/SQ financial thresholds (turnover requirements, balance sheet tests) structurally exclude the SME or require mitigation (e.g., parent company guarantee, consortium, partnering)
- Evaluate whether past contract value requirements in the evaluation criteria create a barrier — SMEs may not have comparable-scale references
- Test whether the buyer values agility, specialism, and direct senior engagement (SME strengths) or requires scale, geographic coverage, and multi-capability delivery (large prime strengths)
- Assess whether social value weighting favours SMEs (local employment, apprenticeship, community impact often score higher for SMEs)
- Consider whether a partnering or sub-contracting route mitigates scale concerns while preserving the SME's differentiation

### Positioning Principles for SME Challengers
- Lead with specialism, sector depth, and direct access to senior expertise
- Position agility and responsiveness as advantages over incumbent bureaucracy
- Be transparent about scale — address it with evidence of how you manage it, not by pretending it is not an issue
- Use social value as a competitive weapon where weighting supports it
- If partnering, show the partner relationship is substantive, not a paper exercise
