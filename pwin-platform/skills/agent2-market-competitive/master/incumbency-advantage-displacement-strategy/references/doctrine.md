## 1. Core Beliefs

These beliefs encode practitioner experience from two decades of complex UK public sector
pursuits. They override generic competitive analysis assumptions.

1. **Incumbency is both a competitor issue and a buyer inertia issue.** The incumbent's strength is only half the picture. Buyer reluctance to change — even when change is rational — is often the decisive factor.

2. **The incumbent may win even when performance is weak if the buyer fears transition risk more than poor service.** This is the single most common reason challengers lose. Do not underestimate it.

3. **The challenger must make switching feel safer than staying.** This is the fundamental positioning requirement for any displacement strategy.

4. **Incumbent advantage is usually operational, relational, behavioural, and evidential — it is not only contractual.** Informal knowledge, workarounds, personal relationships, and embedded routines create stickiness that contracts do not capture.

5. **A re-procurement does not automatically mean dissatisfaction with the incumbent.** Procurements happen for regulatory compliance, political mandate, value-for-money testing, contract expiry, or organisational change. Many result in the incumbent retaining.

6. **Requirements that closely mirror the current operating model may signal incumbent advantage or buyer risk aversion.** If the specification reads like a description of today's service, test whether it has been shaped by or for the incumbent.

7. **Displacement strategy must be framed as evolution, improvement, and continuity of outcomes — not criticism.** Evaluators score the future-state proposition, not the degree of incumbent failure alleged.

8. **Negative intelligence should normally shape internal strategy, not appear directly in the proposal.** Use what you know about incumbent weakness to position your strengths. Do not attack.

9. **Every incumbent vulnerability must be converted into a positive, future-state proposition.** "They have slow reporting" becomes "Our real-time management information provides [specific benefit]."

10. **A weak incumbent creates an opportunity only if the challenger can offer a credible, lower-risk alternative.** Displacement requires both push (incumbent weakness) and pull (challenger strength). Push alone is insufficient.

11. **TUPE, mobilisation, data migration, systems dependency, and knowledge transfer are go/no-go factors.** These are not secondary delivery details. They are often the reason buyers stay with incumbents they would otherwise leave.

12. **A supplier that is the incumbent must not rely on continuity alone.** It must evidence learning, improvement, investment, and next-phase value. Complacent incumbents lose to challengers who offer a vision.

13. **Strong public evidence is more reliable than anecdotal internal sales commentary.** Internal intelligence is valuable for hypothesis generation but must not be treated as fact without corroboration.

14. **Internal intelligence can be valuable but must be labelled as unverified unless validated by source evidence.** The claim labelling system (below) exists to enforce this discipline.

15. **The bid team must understand both why the buyer might change and why the buyer might stay.** A one-sided analysis — either overconfident or overcautious — is a quality failure.

16. **Security clearance is a material switching barrier in Defence, Justice, and national security contracts.** If the incumbent's delivery team holds SC or DV clearance, and the challenger's proposed team does not, mobilisation timelines of 6–12 months create a structural disadvantage that must be factored into transition risk.

17. **Social value commitments create incumbent advantage where evaluation weighting exceeds 10%.** Existing local employment, apprenticeship programmes, community relationships, and supply chain commitments are difficult for a challenger to replicate credibly at bid stage.

18. **Framework membership and rules constrain displacement dynamics.** If the procurement is a call-off from a framework (CCS, Crown Commercial Service, DPS), assess whether framework rules limit lot switching, whether the incumbent's framework position gives them structural advantage, and whether framework expiry creates a natural displacement window.

## 2. Displacement Equation

This is the core strategic test. Apply it throughout every assessment.

```
buyer_dissatisfaction + credible_alternative + acceptable_transition_risk + compelling_value
MUST BE GREATER THAN
incumbent_familiarity + switching_cost + relationship_loyalty + buyer_fear
```

If the left side is not stronger than the right side, the opportunity is likely no-go,
selective-go, or go-with-conditions.

Each element maps to scoring dimensions:

| Equation Element | Scoring Dimension(s) |
|---|---|
| buyer_dissatisfaction | incumbent_performance_strength (inverse), incumbent_vulnerability |
| credible_alternative | challenger_differentiation |
| acceptable_transition_risk | switching_complexity (inverse) |
| compelling_value | challenger_differentiation, commercial advantage |
| incumbent_familiarity | relationship_embeddedness, operational_stickiness |
| switching_cost | switching_complexity, TUPE_and_workforce_complexity |
| relationship_loyalty | relationship_embeddedness, buyer_change_appetite (inverse) |
| buyer_fear | buyer_change_appetite (inverse), requirements_bias_to_incumbent |

## 3. Evidence Hierarchy

Apply this hierarchy when assessing the reliability of any claim about the incumbent,
buyer, or competitive position. Higher tiers override lower tiers when they conflict.

### Tier 1 — Highest Reliability
**Examples:** Procurement documents, tender specifications, award notices, contract award data,
published KPIs, official contract modifications, formal evaluation criteria.

**Treatment:** Treat as primary evidence, subject to date validity and relevance to the
current scope. Evidence older than the staleness threshold (see below) requires explicit
justification for inclusion.

### Tier 2 — High Reliability
**Examples:** National Audit Office reports, Public Accounts Committee reports, regulator
reports, local authority scrutiny reports, board papers, cabinet or committee papers,
inspectorate reports (HMICFRS, Ofsted, CQC, etc.).

**Treatment:** Treat as high-value evidence for performance, risk, governance, service failure,
or strategic context. Cross-reference with Tier 1 where possible.

### Tier 3 — Medium-High Reliability
**Examples:** FOI responses, published service performance reports, public minutes, buyer
strategy documents, transformation roadmaps, annual reports.

**Treatment:** Use as strong contextual evidence, checking date, scope, and whether findings
relate to the incumbent service specifically (not the buyer's broader operations).

### Tier 4 — Medium Reliability
**Examples:** Credible news coverage, specialist trade press, parliamentary questions, public
stakeholder comments.

**Treatment:** Use cautiously. Validate against official sources where possible. Do not
overstate conclusions from media reporting alone. Note that parliamentary questions may
signal political attention rather than confirmed problems.

### Tier 5 — Medium-Low Reliability
**Examples:** Supplier case studies, supplier press releases, marketing material, conference
presentations.

**Treatment:** Useful for understanding supplier positioning and claimed strengths, but these
are self-reported. Not sufficient evidence of buyer satisfaction, delivery quality, or
contract performance.

### Tier 6 — Internal Intelligence
**Examples:** Sales notes, relationship comments, pre-market feedback, incumbent reputation
comments, informal buyer feedback.

**Treatment:** Valuable for hypothesis generation and shaping questions. Label as unverified
unless corroborated by Tier 1–4 evidence. Do not convert directly into bid claims or
factual assertions.

### Tier 7 — Inference
**Examples:** Assumptions from contract extensions, assumptions from requirement design,
assumptions from buyer language, assumptions from procurement timing.

**Treatment:** Clearly label as inference. Use to guide questions, red-team analysis, or
strategy hypotheses — never as fact. The judgement heuristics in
`references/scenarios-and-scoring.md` provide guidance on common inference patterns.

## 4. Claim Labels

Every material claim in the assessment must carry one of these labels:

| Label | Definition | Permitted Use in Bid |
|---|---|---|
| `evidenced_fact` | Supported by Tier 1–3 evidence with a specific source reference | Can inform direct bid claims if relevant |
| `informed_inference` | Logically derived from available evidence but not directly stated in any source | Can shape positioning and narrative themes. Must not be stated as fact. |
| `unverified_intelligence` | Based on Tier 6 (internal) sources or single Tier 4–5 sources without corroboration | Internal strategy use only. Must not appear in bid language. |
| `open_question` | A gap in knowledge where the answer would materially affect the assessment | Generate as a clarification question or intelligence-gathering action |
| `requires_human_validation` | A sensitive claim that should be validated by the pursuit lead, capture manager, or subject matter expert before use | Do not use in any output until validated |

### Worked Examples

**Same data point, different labels depending on source:**

*"The incumbent's SLA performance has declined over the past 18 months."*

- If stated in a published service performance report (Tier 3): `evidenced_fact`
- If mentioned by a sales contact after a buyer meeting (Tier 6): `unverified_intelligence`
- If inferred from a 12-month contract extension rather than the expected 3-year renewal (Tier 7): `informed_inference`
- If a newspaper reports "sources say performance has slipped" (Tier 4, single source): `unverified_intelligence`
- If an NAO report notes "service levels have not consistently met contractual standards" (Tier 2): `evidenced_fact`

**When to use `requires_human_validation`:**

- Intelligence that names specific individuals or implies personal criticism
- Claims about incumbent financial distress that could be market-sensitive
- Assertions about buyer intent based on private conversations
- Any claim that, if wrong, would materially damage the bidder's credibility or relationship

## 5. Evidence Staleness Rules

Evidence ages. A 2019 finding and a 2025 finding are not equally relevant, even if both
come from Tier 2 sources. Apply these rules:

| Evidence Age | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Less than 12 months | Current. Use without age caveat. |
| 12–24 months | Recent. Use with a note that conditions may have changed. |
| 24–36 months | Ageing. Use only if no more recent evidence exists. State the age and note that the finding may no longer reflect current performance. |
| More than 36 months | Stale. Do not use as primary evidence for current performance. May be used for historical context (e.g., "the incumbent was appointed following a 2018 audit that found...") but not for current assessment. |

**Exception:** Contract award dates, procurement routes, and structural facts (e.g., "the contract was originally let in 2016") do not age in the same way as performance evidence. Apply staleness rules to performance, satisfaction, and relationship claims — not to factual contract history.

**In practice:** If the most recent performance evidence is 30 months old and nothing more recent is available, use it — but reduce the confidence rating for that dimension and add an open question: "Updated performance evidence is required to validate this assessment."
