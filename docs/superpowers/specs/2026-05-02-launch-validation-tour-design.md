# BidEquity launch validation tour — conversation playbook design

## Purpose

Design the artefact that supports a structured pre-launch validation tour. The tour is Paul's last calibration window before the public LinkedIn announcement of BidEquity. After the announcement, retracting or pivoting the public message becomes materially more expensive, so the tour exists to surface anything that should change *before* the message is committed.

## What the tour is testing

The tour has three test purposes, in priority order:

1. **Validation** — does the proposition hold up under honest scrutiny from people who can think clearly about it.
2. **Recruitment** — does anyone get sufficiently excited to come on board as a co-founder, director, partner, or route-to-market collaborator.
3. **Pivot signal** — is there a market opportunity adjacent to the one Paul is building toward, that the synthesis of conversations would suggest he should redirect into.

The framing he commits to using in every conversation is *"help me solve a problem here"*, not *"here's what I built, what do you think?"*. The shift is from pitch to inquiry; from explaining to thinking together.

## What the tour cannot fully test (the diagnostic finding)

The thing Paul is most worried about — the commercial story standing up to a CFO-grade examination of the £3bn / £2bn moat thesis and the CFO model — cannot be fully tested by his trusted circle. The right reviewer for that anxiety isn't in the audience. This is acknowledged in the design via two parallel actions:

- A **self-administered CFO stress-test** done before the tour begins (Section 5).
- A **soft introduction ask** included in every tour conversation: *"do you know anyone in a CFO or FD seat at a strategic supplier I could put this in front of?"*.

The tour cannot fully de-risk the commercial story on its own. Pretending it can is the bigger risk than the gap itself.

## The two existential questions underneath the tour

These are surfaced from the brainstorm and reframe what the tour is for:

1. **The credibility configuration.** "Paul built the platform himself" is not survivable in front of a strategic supplier buyer. What founding-team or leadership picture would make the platform's existence plausible — co-founder, board, partner firm, route-to-market arrangement, or some combination?
2. **The trigger to change.** A top-40 strategic supplier may read £2bn waste as "yes, but that's how this industry works, we've already absorbed it." The CFO maths land *and don't matter*, because the buying decision isn't running through the CFO. What would actually trigger a leadership team to change behaviour?

The four questions in the playbook (Section 2) are the operational shape of these two existential questions.

## Audiences

Three primary audiences, plus one parallel track:

| Code | Audience | Primary value to the tour |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Industry insiders — ex-clients, ex-colleagues, anyone who's lived inside a strategic supplier's bid function | Best placed to answer the *trigger* question. Will challenge the methodology and the proprietary asset claim. |
| **B** | Senior business non-industry — friends and peers who run businesses, sit on boards, advise. Smart and commercial, not steeped in UK public sector procurement. | Best placed to think clearly about the *credibility configuration*. May know people who could be on the journey. |
| **C** | Potential commercial buyers — Bid Directors, Capture Directors, COOs at strategic suppliers. Trusted enough to give honest pre-launch feedback. | Tests whether the proposition is real to a buyer. Tests the trigger hypothesis from the buyer side. Highest stakes — should be seen *last*, not first. |
| **D (parallel)** | Potential co-founders, directors, partners, route-to-market collaborators — names surfaced in waves 1 and 2. | Recruitment, not validation. Same conversation shape, different exit ask. Run opportunistically. |

## Deliverable: the conversation playbook

The artefact is a **conversation playbook**, built for Paul, not for the audience. It exists in two forms:

- **Primary: HTML document** — kept open on the laptop during conversations or printed for hand-annotation over coffee. Not for the audience to see.
- **Secondary: slide deck** — same content as the substantive overview chunks (Section 3 below), available as visual scaffolding when the conversation calls for it, or as a leave-behind / send-after asset.

Both share the same source content. They are not separate documents in spirit — they are two presentations of one underlying artefact.

## Section 1 — Playbook structure

The HTML playbook contains five things, in this order:

1. **The framing one-pager** — the literal opening paragraph (Section 2 below) and a short reminder of the three test purposes, so that when Paul sits down he remembers this is an inquiry, not a pitch.
2. **The substantive overview** — the five chunks (Section 3) Paul walks through in the middle of the conversation. The slide deck mirrors this content.
3. **The four real questions** — held loosely, not asked in fixed order (Section 2).
4. **The deeper-dive asset shelf** — bookmarks Paul can pull up live if the conversation goes there (Section 5).
5. **The capture template** — the structured note Paul fills in within 30 minutes of the conversation ending, plus a synthesis log that rolls up across conversations (Section 5).

The CFO stress-test memo (Section 5) sits **outside** the playbook — it's prep work done before the tour begins, not a runtime asset.

## Section 2 — Framing, the four real questions, three exit asks

### The opening paragraph (delivered as written, every conversation)

> *"I'm not here to pitch you anything. I've spent a year and a half building something — a methodology and a platform for how strategic suppliers in UK public sector should pursue their bids — and I'm a few weeks away from making it public. Before I do, I want to spend an hour with people I trust, including you, on three things I genuinely don't know the answer to. I'd value you pushing back hard rather than being kind."*

The point of this paragraph is what it forecloses: it makes flattery awkward, makes "I'm interested, tell me more" socially unsatisfying as a response, and makes it acceptable for them to disagree. It kills the "this is a sales pitch" frame before it forms.

### The four real questions

In priority order. Paul does not ask all four in every conversation. He asks the two or three most fitting for who's in front of him, and he lets the conversation move between them organically.

| # | The question | Primary audience | What to listen for |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | What founding-team / leadership configuration would make this platform survive a strategic supplier's "who else is behind this?" question? | All three, B best-placed | Specific patterns: CTO with platform pedigree, recognisable name on the board, partner firm fronting the route to market. Names mentioned spontaneously. |
| 2 | What would actually trigger a top-40 strategic supplier's leadership team to change how they pursue bids? | A best-placed; C tests it from the buyer side | Trigger candidates: competitor-adopts, regulatory shift, revenue/win-rate pressure, board-driven cost-out programme, new commercial leadership arriving. Reactions to the "challenger eats their breakfast" hypothesis. |
| 3 | Who do you know — for either side of this? | All three | Two distinct asks: (a) people who could help build it (co-founder, director, partner), (b) people who could buy it or open a buying conversation. Names, even tentative. |
| 4 | What am I missing? If you were me, would you redirect this entirely? | All three | Pivot signals. Adjacent markets, adjacent products, adjacent business models. |

### The three exit asks

At the end of the conversation, depending on how it has gone, Paul has three asks available. He picks one — never plants more than one cold:

- **Introduction.** *"Who could you put me in front of for this?"*
- **Involvement.** *"Is this something you'd want to be part of in some shape?"* — only when the conversation has clearly gone there.
- **Pressure-test loop.** *"Can I come back to you in three months when this is in the market and tell you what happened?"* — keeps them on the journey and makes the next conversation easy to get.

## Section 3 — The substantive overview (five chunks)

The middle of the conversation. Roughly 5–10 minutes of Paul talking, modular so he can shorten or expand depending on what's already landed from the website pre-read. Each chunk is one slide in the deck and one short page-section in the HTML.

**Chunk 1 — The problem.** *The £3bn / £2bn moat thesis, in three sentences.*
> The top 40 strategic suppliers to UK public sector spend roughly £3bn a year on bid pursuit. By industry estimate, around £2bn of that goes on bids they were never going to win. The cost isn't just the bid budgets — it's the opportunity cost of capture teams chasing the wrong things, the reputational drag of repeated losses, and the executive credibility lost when over-confident win-probability scores prove unfounded.

The point of leading here is to surface — early — the possibility that the audience does not see this as a problem. If the chunk gets pushback, the trigger question (Question 2) has just been opened naturally and the rest of the overview can be compressed.

**Chunk 2 — Why nobody's solved it yet.** *The diagnosis.*
> Existing approaches to win-probability scoring are either too subjective (Happy Ears — sales optimism inflating scores), too academic (impractical at the speed of decision-making), or too simplistic (single weighted scorecards dressed up as algorithms). None combine algorithmic discipline, capture evidence, structural reality, and bias correction in one system. The US-focused tools — Cleat.ai, pWin.ai — are scoring tools, not challenge functions. They tell you the score; they don't tell you to walk away.

**Chunk 3 — What I've built.** *The platform, named honestly.* Two parts:

- **The methodology.** The Pre-Gate Pursuit Playbook (S1–S5) — Opportunity Sense-Making, Buyer & Field Shaping, Win Architecture Design, Commercial Risk & Suitability Review, Commitment Readiness. Then bid execution.
- **The platform.** Six products: Qualify (the assurance and challenge layer), Win Strategy (the capture playbook engine), Bid Execution (the production discipline), Verdict (post-loss forensic review), Portfolio (the leadership dashboard), Competitive Intel (the proprietary UK contracts data layer behind everything else — 175,000 contracts, master-listed buyers and suppliers, framework intelligence, stakeholder map).

Honest register. No "AI-powered SaaS." No "next-generation."

**Chunk 4 — The proprietary asset behind it.** *The closest thing to a moat.*
> The five-year, 175,000-contract UK public sector procurement database, with master-listed buyers, master-listed suppliers, framework holders, executive organograms, and a confidence catalogue describing exactly what the data can and cannot tell us. Built natively, not licensed. UK-specific, not adapted from a US system. Updated nightly. This is what makes the multi-layered probability score possible.

This chunk lands hardest with audience A (they know what doesn't exist in the UK). It lands lightest with audience B (they can't calibrate the rarity).

**Chunk 5 — The honest state.** *What's true today, what's not yet, where the soft spots are.* This is the chunk that distinguishes the conversation from a pitch:

- **Working today:** the contracts intelligence database, Qualify (live, lead-gen ready), the methodology, the founder credibility, the website.
- **Built but not yet battle-tested on a live engagement:** Win Strategy and Bid Execution. The methodology hasn't run on a paid client end-to-end yet.
- **Unknown:** whether strategic suppliers will move on this absent a forcing function. Whether the credibility configuration around me is sufficient. Whether the commercial model is the right shape.

This chunk is what creates the conditions for the four questions. By the time it has been said, the audience already understands they're being invited into a real working problem.

### Audience modulation of the overview

| Audience | Linger on | Compress |
|---|---|---|
| A (industry insiders) | Chunks 3 and 4 (methodology + proprietary asset) | Chunk 1 |
| B (senior business non-industry) | Chunks 1 and 5 (problem + honest state) | Chunks 3 and 4 |
| C (potential commercial buyers) | Chunks 1 and 3 (problem + platform) | Chunk 4 |

## Section 4 — Conversation flow (60 minutes)

Four phases, time-boxed. No fixed script inside any phase.

| Phase | Duration | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Opening + consent | 0–4 min | The framing paragraph, delivered as written. Then a beat of silence. Then the recording-consent ask: *"Before we start — I'd like to record this so I can listen back properly afterwards rather than scribbling notes. The recording stays with me, gets transcribed for my own synthesis, and isn't published anywhere. Is that OK with you?"* If yes, start recording. If no, fall back to written notes — the conversation still happens, no friction. |
| Substantive overview | 4–16 min | The five chunks, modulated for the audience. Paul watches them while he talks; if a chunk visibly lands or visibly bores them, he compresses or expands on the fly. The slide / HTML page-section is open between them as a visual anchor, not a teleprompter. |
| The four questions | 16–55 min | Two or three of the four, ordered organically. Silence is welcome. If they offer a name, Paul writes it down then. If they push back on the trigger hypothesis, he lets them push. |
| Close | 55–60 min | Three things, in order: (1) *"What's the single thing you'd want me to think hardest about between now and when this goes public?"* — the distilled takeaway. (2) The fitting exit ask, with a committed next step on the spot. (3) A genuine, specific thank-you. Stop the recording before standing up. |

### Pre-read strategy

The website is the pre-read. No bespoke pre-read material is created. The framing email — three sentences max, sent 48–72 hours before the conversation — calibrates the audience to arrive at the right altitude. Three variants:

**Audience A (industry insiders) — lead with the puzzle and the proprietary asset.**
> *"I've spent 18 months building a methodology and a platform for how strategic suppliers in UK public sector should pursue bids — including a five-year, 175,000-contract intelligence database. Before I take it public next month, I want to spend an hour with people who know how this market actually works. The site is at bidequity.co.uk if you want a 10-minute scan beforehand — particularly the founder interview at /perspective. I'll talk you through what's behind it, and then I want to ask you three or four hard questions I genuinely don't know the answers to. No pitch."*

**Audience B (senior business non-industry) — lead with the founder position and the human invitation.**
> *"I'm a few weeks away from launching a business — BidEquity — that I've been building quietly for the last 18 months. Before I do, I want to spend an hour with people whose judgement I trust, including yours. There are two or three questions I genuinely don't know the answer to that you might help me think through. The website is at bidequity.co.uk if you want a quick look — the founder interview at /perspective is the closest thing to a manifesto. No pitch, no slides, just a conversation."*

**Audience C (potential commercial buyers) — lead with the asymmetry: this is not a sales conversation.**
> *"I'm getting close to launching a business focused on how strategic suppliers like yours pursue UK public sector bids. Before I do, I'm taking an hour with a small number of senior people on the buyer side — not to sell to them, but to ask whether they think the proposition holds up, and what would have to be true for a firm like theirs to entertain it. Worth ten minutes of your time on bidequity.co.uk first if you're willing. I'd value the honest read."*

The variation is in register, not content: A wants to know Paul respects their domain expertise; B wants to know this is human and unrehearsed; C wants to know Paul is not about to sell to them.

## Section 5 — Reserves: asset shelf, capture, CFO prep

### Deeper-dive asset shelf

Bookmarks held in the HTML playbook, surfaced live only if a conversation earns them. Discipline: no more than two reserve assets shown in any one conversation. A third means Paul has stopped listening.

| Asset | Surface when they ask | Form |
|---|---|---|
| The CFO model | "Where do those numbers come from?" / "What does the client maths actually look like?" | Interactive HTML model (`pwin-competitive-intel/bidequity-cfo-model.html`) — open it, change the slider, show them. |
| The five-layer PWIN explainer | "How is this different from a scorecard?" | One page diagramming Historical Pattern, Qualify Assessment, Capture Evidence, Cognitive Discount, Structural Ceiling — built fresh for this artefact, lifted from the wiki concept page. |
| Live Qualify walkthrough | "Show me how it actually works" / curious C-audience buyer | Open `bidequity.co.uk/qualify-app.html` and run a 3-minute scoring on a real opportunity in front of them. |
| Data confidence catalogue | "How honest are you about what the data can and can't tell you?" — typically from audience A | One page with the confidence-graded list of what the contracts database can and cannot honestly assert. The fact it exists matters more than what's on it. |
| Pre-Gate Pursuit Playbook diagram | "What's the methodology, exactly?" | One page with S1 → S5 visualised, the four workstreams alongside, and the artefact each stage produces. |
| Founder interview | "Tell me about you" / when credibility comes up directly | Already on the website at `/perspective/founder-interview` — Paul points them at it as a follow-up read rather than rehearsing it verbally. |

### Capture and synthesis system

**Primary path: recorded conversation → transcript → AI synthesis to structured Markdown.**

The recording is captured on Paul's phone or laptop with explicit consent given at the start of the conversation (see Section 4). It is transcribed (via Otter, Notta, MacWhisper, or equivalent — tool choice is Paul's, not specified by the spec). The transcript is then synthesised by AI into the structured Markdown template below, using a fixed synthesis prompt held at `launch-validation/templates/synthesis-prompt.md`. The synthesised file is committed (or kept local — see privacy convention below); the transcript and audio are workings, kept local and purged after synthesis is reviewed.

**Fallback path: spreadsheet template, filled by hand.** Used when the audience declines recording, when the environment makes recording impractical (noisy venue, video call with audio issues), or when Paul prefers to scribble. Same structure as the Markdown template, in spreadsheet form. One row per conversation, filled within 30 minutes of the conversation ending.

**Per-conversation Markdown template (`launch-validation/templates/conversation-capture.md`).**

Fixed structure, one file per conversation, named `YYYY-MM-DD-<initials>-<audience-code>.md`:

- **Header** — who, when, audience type (A/B/C/D), recording status (recorded / written notes), duration.
- **One-line takeaway from their perspective** — what they'd say BidEquity is, in their own words. If the synthesis can't produce this, the recording wasn't listened to closely enough.
- **Reaction by chunk (1–5)** — green/amber/red on each, with one-line evidence (verbatim quote where available).
- **Per-question reaction** — what they actually said, verbatim quotes preferred, against questions 1–4 (those that came up).
- **Pivot signals** — anything suggesting the proposition or model needs rethinking, with the verbatim trigger quote.
- **Names mentioned** — both directions: people offered for introduction, people suggested as co-founders/directors/partners. Live list across the whole tour, never filed.
- **Exit ask agreed** — what Paul committed to do, by when.
- **Single-thing takeaway** — their answer to *"what should I think hardest about?"*, verbatim.
- **Quotes worth keeping** — 3–5 verbatim quotes that capture the texture of the conversation. Useful later for refining the substantive overview language.

**Synthesis prompt (`launch-validation/templates/synthesis-prompt.md`).**

A fixed instruction set that converts a raw transcript into the Markdown template above. The prompt names the four real questions explicitly, names the five overview chunks, and instructs the AI to extract verbatim quotes wherever possible rather than paraphrase. The point is reproducibility — every conversation gets synthesised the same way, so the synthesis log can be read as comparable evidence rather than as differently-toned summaries. The prompt is built once as part of the artefact build and refined in-flight if early conversations show it missing things.

**Synthesis log (`launch-validation/templates/synthesis-log.csv` and `launch-validation/synthesis-log.md`).**

Rolls up across conversations. One row per conversation, columns mirroring the per-conversation template (audience, takeaway, R/A/G per chunk, names mentioned, exit ask, single-thing takeaway). The Markdown version is the human-readable index that links to each per-conversation file. The signal threshold: three different people from three different audiences flagging the same gap is real; one person flagging it is noise. Paul reviews the synthesis log every 3–4 conversations, not after every one — too-frequent review tempts premature pivots.

**Privacy convention.**

- Raw audio and raw transcripts are **gitignored** by default. They contain names, opinions, and commercially sensitive views from people who agreed to be recorded for Paul's own synthesis, not for repository commit.
- Synthesised per-conversation Markdown files contain names and verbatim quotes too. Default convention: **gitignored**. The synthesis log can either be (a) kept local-only, or (b) committed in an anonymised form (audience type only, no initials, no third-party names). The implementation plan should pick one; the spec leaves both available.
- The `.gitignore` in `launch-validation/` is built as part of the artefact and excludes raw recordings, transcripts, and per-conversation synthesised files by default.

### CFO stress-test prep (out-of-band)

Done before any conversation starts. Self-administered, cold and forensic. Deliverable: a one-page memo where Paul states the key claims of the £3bn / £2bn moat thesis and the CFO model, and writes the case against each one.

Specific cross-examination points to address:

- Where does the £3bn industry spend figure come from? What's the underlying derivation? What does it not include? What would a hostile reviewer say is missing or double-counted?
- Where does the £2bn waste figure come from? What's the survey assumption? What would change it to £1bn or £3bn?
- What's the per-pursuit ROI claim, and at what win rate, at what margin profile, with what bid spend rate? Where is the calibration weakest? IT consulting / engineering consulting / management consulting bid-spend rates are estimates pending real consulting client P&L data — name that explicitly.
- What's the company-level ROI claim, and what's the difference between per-pursuit (about 0.9×) and portfolio (about 3.2×)? Is that distinction visible in the model, or does it look like double-counting to a fresh reader?

Output is not a defence — it's a list of *known soft spots*, each with either a fix or an explicit acknowledgement of "this is a soft spot we'll honestly admit." When Paul hands the model to a finance professional, he walks them through the soft spots first. That is what disarms cross-examination.

## Section 6 — Sequencing and pace

### Principles

1. **Earlier conversations sharpen the proposition; later conversations stress-test the sharpened version.** Paul should not be saying the same thing in conversation eleven that he said in conversation one. If the artefact isn't changing across waves, he isn't listening.
2. **The audience he most fears is the one he runs last, not first.** Audience C is highest-stakes and hardest to redo. They should see v3 of the story, not v1.
3. **The credibility configuration question needs at least a draft answer in his back pocket before he sits in front of a commercial buyer.** Audience B (which helps form that answer) precedes Audience C (which tests it).

### Wave sequence

| Wave | Audience | Approx count | What to listen for | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wave 1 — Friendly stress test | B | 3–4 | Reactions to credibility configuration. Names of partners/directors/co-founders. Honest read on founder positioning. Where the story is glib. | Weeks 1–2 |
| Wave 1.5 — Revision pass | — | — | Revise overview, framing, questions. Draft credibility configuration as it would sound to a buyer. CFO stress-test if not yet done. | Weekend after Wave 1 |
| Wave 2 — Domain stress test | A | 4–6 | Reactions to trigger question. Reactions to methodology and proprietary asset claim. Names on either side. Whether BidEquity is meaningfully different from Shipley. | Weeks 3–4 |
| Wave 2.5 — Revision pass | — | — | Trigger hypothesis sharpens or breaks. Pivot question may genuinely come into play. | Weekend after Wave 2 |
| Wave 3 — Buyer-side reality check | C | 3–5 | Whether the proposition is real to them. What would actually move them. Whether the trigger hypotheses hold up against the buyer's self-account. | Weeks 5–6 |
| Wave 3.5 — Synthesis and decision | — | — | Full read of synthesis log. Three decisions: (a) does proposition hold up or pivot; (b) credibility configuration committed to; (c) public launch on or off. | Week 7 |

**Audience D (recruitment track) runs in parallel, opportunistically.** Whenever Wave 1 or Wave 2 surfaces a name worth pursuing, Paul books that conversation independently. By week 7 he may have 2–4 of these — the right size to begin a real founding-team conversation with one or two of them.

### Pace and density

- 10–15 conversations across 6–7 weeks. Two per week comfortable; three is a fortnight stretch.
- Don't double-book a day. The 30-minute synthesis happens after, and gets cannibalised when rushing to the next coffee.
- Revision passes are protected time. They're where the value of the tour compounds.

### Decision gate at week 7

The fixed deliverable. Paul writes a one-page memo to himself answering three questions:

1. Does the proposition hold up, or am I pivoting? If pivoting, which direction does the synthesis log point at?
2. What's the credibility configuration I'm committing to for launch — and what's the gap to fill before launch (named people, target roles, rough timeline)?
3. Is the public LinkedIn launch on or off, and if on, is there anything in the live website that needs to change first?

This memo is what the tour is for. Everything else is in service of writing it well.

## Success criteria

The design is successful if, by week 7, Paul has:

- Run 10–15 conversations across audiences A, B, C, plus 2–4 with audience D (recruitment).
- A synthesis log showing pattern-level signal across the conversations.
- A revised, sharpened version of the substantive overview that has demonstrably changed across the three waves.
- A draft credibility configuration he can describe to a buyer.
- A one-page decision memo answering the three week-7 questions.
- A clear go / no-go / pivot call on the public LinkedIn launch.
