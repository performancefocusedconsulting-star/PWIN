01

Product Overview

PWIN Coach is an AI-assisted bid qualification and coaching application. It enables pursuit teams to self-assess the strength of an opportunity across six evidence-based categories, receive an objective probability-of-win (PWIN) score, and get real-time AI challenge and coaching on the quality of their evidence.

The product is intentionally positioned as a coaching and validation tool, not a data entry form. Its primary value is forcing honest articulation of capture position — the AI layer exists to prevent score inflation and surface genuine gaps.

Core Positioning Decision
PWIN Coach is an AI capture director — the qualification assessment is one of its tools, not the product itself. Assets it helps build are the deliverables. The coaching relationship over time is the value.
02

Application Structure & Tabs

The application is structured as a four-tab single-page application. Tab order is deliberate — it reflects the logical sequence of a pursuit capture review. The Context tab was added after the initial prototype specifically to improve AI output quality.

TAB 1
Context
Opportunity metadata form. Captures fields that inform the AI's context window for every review call. Mandatory — Assessment tab is locked until complete.
⚑ Gate: must complete before proceeding
TAB 2
Assessment
24-question scoring interface grouped by 6 categories. Each question has a position dropdown, evidence textarea, and an inline AI review button.
TAB 3
Dashboard
Live PWIN gauge, category contribution bars, radar/spider chart, and completion indicator. Updates in real-time as Assessment is completed.
TAB 4
AI Review
Full AI assurance report. Runs all 24 questions through the AI in a single pass. Returns structured verdicts, inflation flags, challenge questions, and capture actions.
Why Tab 1 (Context) was added: The original prototype had no context — the AI reviewed evidence without knowing the sector, contract value, procurement route, or incumbent position. This produced generic feedback. Adding the context tab enables the AI to calibrate its evidence bar to TCV, apply sector-specific procurement norms, and reference the organisation and opportunity by name in its challenge questions.
03

Scoring Model

Each of the 24 questions uses a four-point Likert scale. The scale was deliberately limited to four points (no neutral midpoint) to force a directional position — either the team has evidence or it doesn't.

Strongly Agree
3
Named evidence, documented commitments, verifiable facts
Agree
2
Reasonable position, some evidence but gaps remain
Disagree
1
Identified gap with a plan to address it
Strongly Disagree
0
No position, no plan — genuine risk
Key Decision — Blank Questions Score Zero
Questions left unanswered are treated as Strongly Disagree (0 points). This was deliberate — blank questions must penalise the score to reflect genuine capture incompleteness. A team cannot achieve a strong PWIN by simply leaving difficult questions blank.
Why this matters: The alternative (treat blanks as neutral or exclude from calculation) would allow teams to game the score by only answering questions where they are strong. Blank = 0 forces honest completion and makes the PWIN score a true reflection of capture readiness, not selective self-reporting. A completion indicator (e.g. "18/24 answered") is shown alongside the PWIN score so a pursuit director can distinguish a genuinely weak team from one that simply hasn't completed the form yet.

Category Weights

Six categories are used, weighted in two tiers (High = 20%, Low = 10%). Weights sum to 100%. The high/low split reflects the degree to which each category is within the pursuit team's control and its proven influence on public sector win/loss outcomes.

Category Tier Weight Rationale
Competitive Positioning High 20% Primary differentiator in contested bids — directly within the team's control
Stakeholder Strength High 20% Critical in public sector — relationships often decide shortlisting outcomes
Value Proposition High 20% Core evaluator influence lever that drives scored criteria responses
Organisational Influence High 20% Often the hidden deciding factor — difficult for competitors to replicate quickly
Procurement Intelligence Low 10% Largely predetermined in public sector procurement frameworks
Pursuit Progress Low 10% A lagging indicator — reflects the outcomes of the four high-weight categories
04

PWIN Calculation Formula

PWIN is calculated in two steps. First, each category score is computed as a weighted contribution. Then PWIN is the sum of all six category contributions. The maximum possible score is 100%.

Step 1 — Category Contribution
Category Contribution = ( Raw Score ÷ Max Possible Score ) × Category Weight
Step 2 — Overall PWIN
PWIN % = Σ ( all six Category Contributions )
Where
Raw Score = weighted sum of question points within category
Max Possible Score = sum of all question weights × 3 (Strongly Agree) for that category
Category Weight = 0.20 (High tier) or 0.10 (Low tier)
Note: Question-level weights within each category are applied internally and not surfaced to the user. They modify which questions contribute most to a category's raw score without changing the visible scoring interface. This keeps the UI simple while allowing fine-grained calibration by the product owner.
PWIN Target
The target PWIN score at ITT submission is 75%. Achieving this requires an average of approximately 9/12 across all categories — i.e. the team must demonstrate Agree on most questions and Strongly Agree on the high-weight ones. This was chosen as the right level of challenge for a well-prepared pursuit team.

The 24 Questions

24 questions are distributed evenly — 4 per category. Each question has a topic label (short name shown in the UI), full question text, a scoring rubric defining what evidence justifies each score level, and a set of inflation signal patterns that trigger AI scrutiny.

Per-question scoring rubrics: Every question has a rubric that defines precisely what evidence is required at each score level. Example for Q9 Internal Champion: Strongly Agree requires a named individual, their seniority, and a documented joint action plan with milestones. Agree requires a named individual and verbal commitment but no formal plan. Disagree means contact identified but no commitment secured. Strongly Disagree means no identified contact at all. The AI uses these rubrics as its primary reference when validating submitted evidence.

Context Tab — Fields & AI Impact

The Context tab captures structured metadata about the opportunity before any scoring begins. These fields are injected into the AI's system prompt on every review call, both single-question reviews and the full assurance report. The tab is mandatory — the Assessment tab is locked until all required fields are complete.

Field Type Required How It Improves AI Output
Organisation Name Free text Mandatory Allows AI to reference the client by name in challenge questions. Public sector bodies have known procurement cultures; the AI can calibrate Stakeholder and Procurement questions accordingly.
Opportunity Name Free text Mandatory Grounds the narrative — challenge questions become "Has [Opportunity Name] been through market engagement?" rather than generic.
Sector Dropdown Mandatory Highest-value field. Emergency Services, Defence, Justice, NHS, Local Gov, Central Gov each have distinct procurement norms, incumbent dynamics, and relationship structures. The AI calibrates inflation signals differently per sector.
TCV (£) Currency input Mandatory Complexity and risk proxy. The AI holds a higher evidence bar at higher TCV — a champion claim sufficient for a £3m contract is inadequate for a £150m programme.
Procurement Route Dropdown Mandatory Open/Restricted/Competitive Dialogue/Direct Award/Framework Call-off dramatically changes what Procurement Intelligence questions mean. A call-off has a different decision dynamic to a full OJEU.
Incumbent Status Dropdown Mandatory One of the most important contextual variables. Incumbents and challengers face opposite risks; the AI weights questions accordingly. Incumbent claims around relationships are held to a higher bar.
Pursuit Stage Dropdown Optional Pre-market / ITT not yet published / ITT live / Shortlisted. Contextualises which PWIN benchmarks apply.
Opportunity Type Dropdown Optional BPO/ITO, Consulting/Advisory, Infrastructure/Hardware, Cloud/SaaS. Tells the AI what is being bought, calibrating its challenge questions per type (e.g. TUPE dynamics for BPO; personal relationship dominance for consulting).
Fields Deliberately Excluded
Client contact names, internal team names, and budget references were excluded. They add data entry friction without meaningfully changing AI output quality. The AI does not need names to improve its analysis — it needs structural context.
07

AI Assurance Layer — Design

The AI layer is the distinguishing feature of PWIN Coach. It acts as an independent bid director at a gate review — not a helpful assistant, but a sceptical challenger whose job is to surface inflation and force honest evidence standards.

Three Rules Layers Embedded in Every Review

LayerWhat It Does
Scoring Rubric Per-question definition of exactly what evidence is required at each score level. The AI's primary reference for validating submitted evidence against claimed score.
Inflation Signal Detector Per-question list of language patterns that trigger automatic scrutiny: future tense presented as current achievement ("we plan to…"), vague assertions ("we have good relationships"), circular reasoning ("we are confident because we are well placed"), absence of named individuals or documented commitments.
Context Block All fields from the Context tab, prepended to every AI call. Enables sector, TCV, procurement route, and incumbent calibration of evidence expectations.

Structured AI Response Format

The AI returns a structured JSON object rather than free-form text. This ensures consistency across all 24 questions and all pursuits, and allows the UI to render the response in a controlled, scannable format.

AI returns JSON with these fields:
"verdict": "Validated" | "Queried" | "Challenged"
"suggestedScore": "Strongly Agree" | "Agree" | "Disagree" | "Strongly Disagree"
"confidenceLevel": number 0–100
"narrative": "2–3 sentence assessment referencing pursuit context"
"inflationDetected": boolean
"inflationReason": "specific language or claim that triggered the flag"
"challengeQuestions": ["q1", "q2", "q3"]
"captureActions": ["action1", "action2"]
The system prompt instructs the AI to:
— Act as a senior bid director at a gate review, not a helpful assistant
— Adjust evidence bar expectations to TCV
— Reference sector-specific procurement culture and norms
— Factor in incumbent vs. challenger position when assessing relationship claims
— Use organisation and opportunity name to make questions specific, not generic
— Consider procurement route when evaluating Procurement Intelligence claims
Why structured JSON, not free text: Free-form AI responses produce variable length, variable tone, and variable structure across reviews. A pursuit director reading 24 free-text blocks cannot easily compare or prioritise. Structured JSON means the UI can always render a verdict badge, an inflation flag, exactly three challenge questions, and exactly two capture actions — creating a consistent review experience regardless of which question or which pursuit is being reviewed.

Content System & Tuning Workflow

The product's scoring intelligence — questions, rubrics, persona, opportunity-type calibration — was originally hard-coded inside each HTML app. As of v0.1.0 it has been externalised into a single versioned JSON file that both Qualify apps load at build time. This unlocks systematic tuning of the AI behaviour without HTML edits, eval-harness regression testing, and version-controlled iteration on the AI scoring layer.

The Two Apps Share One Scoring Brain

AppPathPurpose
Consulting standalone (canon) pwin-qualify/docs/PWIN_Architect_v1.html The main product going forward. Carries the full intelligence stack (intel injection, deeper context, rebid module enabled).
Website MVP bidequity-co/qualify-app.html Public lead-gen, deliberately under-enriched. Stripped variant — same scoring brain, simpler presentation, no intel injection.

Both apps load QUALIFY_CONTENT from pwin-qualify/content/qualify-content-vX.Y.json. The build script (pwin-qualify/content/build-content.js) injects the JSON between sentinel markers in each HTML file. Brand colours stay app-local — they are the only intentional drift between the two apps' scoring layers.

What Lives in the Content File

SectionWhat it controls
questionPacks.standardThe 24 standard questions: text, rubric (4 bands), inflation signals (per-question), normalised within-category weight
modifiers.incumbentThe rebid modifier layer (see next section)
categories + weightProfiles + oppTypeProfileThe six standard categories and the per-opportunity-type weight rebalancing
personaAlex Mercer's full persona object: identity, beliefs, traits, tone, language patterns, hard rules, workflow triggers, success criteria
opportunityTypeCalibrationThe seven opportunity-type-specific calibration paragraphs injected into Alex's prompt at runtime
outputsOutput schema definitions referenced by the apps

The Four Tunable Areas

Per the post-MVP fine-tuning intent, four areas are designed for iteration. Question and category weights are explicitly stable.

The Publishing Workflow

1. Edit pwin-qualify/content/qualify-content-vX.Y.json
2. Bump version + add changelog entry
3. Run eval harness — node pwin-qualify/content/eval-harness.js
4. Review the diff manually (eyeball at least one Alex review per affected fixture)
5. Run build script — node pwin-qualify/content/build-content.js
6. Commit JSON + both HTML files together

Eval Harness

The eval harness (pwin-qualify/content/eval-harness.js) runs each fixture in eval-fixtures/ through the live API and diffs Alex's response against the fixture's expected verdict, inflation flag, and suggested score. Use it before publishing any new content version. Add a fixture for every meaningful tune so the rule doesn't regress next time you change something else.

Cost is roughly $0.01–$0.02 per fixture with Sonnet 4.6. A full run of 10–15 fixtures stays well under $0.30. Seed fixtures from the rebid workbook few-shots are flagged isConstructed: true and should be replaced with real BIP cases when available.

Why externalised, not hard-coded: The product's scoring intelligence is the most valuable and most frequently tuned part of the system. Hard-coded literals make every tune a HTML edit, risk regression with every change, and force the consulting and website variants to drift unless someone manually keeps them in sync. A versioned content file with a build script and an eval harness gives the editor a single point of truth, fast feedback, and rollback safety. The cost is one extra build step. Worth it.

Rebid Modifier Layer

The rebid modifier is the proof case for the modifier mechanism. When the user selects "We are the incumbent defending this contract" in the Context tab, a 12-question incumbent layer activates on top of the standard 24, additional persona triggers extend Alex's calibration, and a separate Rebid Risk Assessment is produced alongside the standard report.

Source: pwin-qualify/docs/PWIN_Rebid_Module_Review.xlsx — a 5-sheet design pack covering principles, questions, risk-assessment schema, trigger rules, and few-shot calibration examples. The JSON content file is generated from this spec.

Why a Modifier, Not a Replacement Pack

The rebid layer is additive, not a substitute. An incumbent's pursuit team still needs the standard 24 questions about competitive positioning, procurement intelligence, stakeholder strength, etc. — a rebid is still a bid. What the standard pack misses is the specific decay risks unique to incumbents: complacency blindness, relationship currency, political map decay, ITT specification influence, challenger threat assessment. The 12 R-questions add those without replacing anything.

What Activates When Incumbent Is Selected

LayerWhat changes
Categories A 7th category — Incumbent Position — is added at 20% weight. The standard six rebalance proportionally to 80% total. PWIN totals stay normalised to 1.
Questions 12 additional questions (R1–R12) are appended, organised into four rebid sub-categories: Contract Performance Reality (R1–R3), Incumbent Position Decay (R4–R6), Retendering Intelligence (R7–R9), Challenger Threat Assessment (R10–R12).
Persona triggers Alex's workflowTriggers object gains 4 inflation triggers (RI-1..RI-4), 4 calibration rules (RC-1..RC-4), and 3 auto-verdict rules (RA-1..RA-3). Each is prefixed with [ref] for traceability when the AI cites which rule fired.
Output schema A new output — the Rebid Risk Assessment — is registered. It has 7 core fields (incumbent vulnerability score, decay risk profile, challenger exploit assessment, retendering motivation, blind spots, strategy recommendation, remediation actions) plus 2 fields originally flagged PROPOSED that are now active (performance narrative gaps, switching cost quantification).

How the Combined Report Works

When the user clicks "Generate Full Assurance Report" with the rebid modifier active, the app merges the Rebid Risk Assessment schema into the standard full-report request. One AI call returns one combined JSON object. The standard report renders as normal; the rebid risk assessment renders as a separate section below the gate recommendation, with its own colour palette (incumbent burgundy) and a Red/Amber/Green decay risk profile.

Deactivation is automatic. If the user changes the radio button to "challenger" or "no clear incumbent", the next call to applyContentModifiers() rebuilds from base — categories return to 6, questions to 24, persona triggers reset, and the rebid output is unregistered. The flip is clean and reversible.

Strategy Recommendation Spectrum

The Rebid Risk Assessment forces Alex to recommend one of four strategic postures, mirroring the gate recommendation pattern from the standard report:

PostureWhen it applies
Defend and ShapeStrong position. Focus on specification influence and transition risk amplification.
Active RecoveryPosition salvageable but requires specific remediation actions before ITT.
Emergency InterventionPosition materially weaker than the team believes. Urgent strategic reset required.
Qualify OutInsufficient position to win at acceptable cost. Recommend early qualification out.
Why the rebid question is reframed: The standard PWIN question is "what is our win probability?" The rebid question is "what do our competitors know that we don't?" Incumbents systematically over-score the standard 24 because they have built relationships, built delivery intelligence, built a value proposition — they answer "yes" to questions about whether they have those things. The rebid layer specifically probes whether those assets are still current, whether the team has tested them in a pursuit context, and whether a well-prepared challenger would expose decay. The most dangerous rebid position is the one that feels safest.

Key Decisions Considered & Rejected

Several design alternatives were discussed and deliberately rejected during the build. These are recorded here so future developers and product owners understand why the current design was chosen and can evaluate whether the constraints that drove those rejections have changed.

Rejected — Dynamic Weights by Opportunity Type
Idea: Change category weights dynamically based on the opportunity type selected in the Context tab (e.g. Consulting pursuits weight Stakeholder Strength higher; Infrastructure bids weight Procurement Intelligence higher).

Why rejected: (1) Dynamic weights destroy comparability — a 65% PWIN on a Consulting pursuit is a fundamentally different number to a 65% on an ITO. You cannot benchmark teams or compare pipeline. (2) Creates a three-layer weighting system (category weights + question weights + type modifiers) that no one, including the product owner, can intuitively explain — pursuit teams will game it by changing the opportunity type to improve their score. (3) Calibration workload: 8 types × 6 categories = 48 adjustments minimum, all of which require real pursuit data to validate and will take 12–18 months to prove.

How the need is met instead: The AI enrichment layer provides contextual adjustment at the right level. When opportunity type is in the context block, the AI already calibrates its challenge questions and evidence bar per type. The PWIN score stays stable and comparable. The AI output is the contextual layer.
Rejected — Blank Questions as Neutral (not zero)
Idea: Exclude unanswered questions from the PWIN calculation (rather than scoring them as 0) so a partial assessment produces a score based only on answered questions.

Why rejected: This would allow pursuit teams to achieve a high PWIN by selectively answering only the questions where they are strong. A team with no champion, no intelligence on procurement, and no competitive strategy could score 80% by only answering the eight questions they're confident about. The score would cease to measure capture readiness and start measuring selective self-reporting.

Design consequence: A completion indicator ("18/24 answered") is shown prominently on the Dashboard tab so a pursuit director can distinguish a team that is genuinely weak (low score + high completion) from one that is simply early in capture (low score + low completion).
Rejected — Including Client Contact Names as a Context Field
Idea: Capture individual stakeholder names in the Context tab for the AI to reference.

Why rejected: Adds data entry friction without meaningfully improving AI output. The AI doesn't need names to calibrate its analysis — it needs structural context (sector, TCV, route, incumbent position). Names in a context block also create a data sensitivity risk if the document is shared or stored.
