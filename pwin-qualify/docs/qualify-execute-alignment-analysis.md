## B. Gap Analysis — Qualify Questions NOT Covered by SAL

These Qualify questions assess things the SAL methodology does not directly address or addresses only tangentially.

### 1. VP-2: Client Value Justification (cost-benefit validation)
**Gap severity: Significant**

The Qualify question asks whether the cost-benefit case has been validated with client stakeholders and impacts confirmed as credible. The SAL workstream contains no activity that explicitly develops or validates a cost-benefit case. This sits in the COM (Commercial) workstream, which is not covered by the SAL methodology mapping. SAL-04 tests win themes against evidence but does not require financial/operational impact modelling or client validation of those impacts.

**Implication:** The Qualify question is probing something real and important, but it falls outside the SAL scope. This is not a methodology gap — it is a cross-workstream dependency that Qualify correctly captures but SAL alone does not deliver.

### 2. PI-1: Clarity of Approval Steps (internal client approval chain)
**Gap severity: Moderate**

The question asks whether the client has shared their full approval and sign-off sequence. SAL-01.1.1 captures the procurement route and milestones, and SAL-10.1.1 identifies the DMU. However, no SAL task explicitly maps the client's internal approval chain (e.g. who signs off at each governance stage, what sequence approvals follow, what conditions trigger re-approval). The methodology assumes the formal procurement process is the decision mechanism, but in practice — especially in public sector — internal approval chains can override or delay formal outcomes.

**Implication:** The Qualify question probes something the methodology under-specifies. A pursuit team could complete all SAL tasks and still not have mapped the client's internal approval sequence.

### 3. PP-3: Message Adoption (client using our language internally)
**Gap severity: Low (by design)**

This is a lagging indicator. No SAL task explicitly measures whether the client has adopted our messaging. It is an outcome of effective SAL-04 (win themes) and SAL-10 (stakeholder engagement) execution, not a task in itself. The methodology correctly treats this as an effect rather than an activity.

**Implication:** No change needed. The Qualify question correctly assesses a leading indicator of capture influence that cannot be produced by a methodology task.

### 4. PP-4: Future Focus (long-term partnership conversations)
**Gap severity: Low (by design)**

Conversations about future phases and extensions are a pursuit momentum signal, not a bid production activity. SAL-06.2.2 touches strategic fit and growth opportunity at a portfolio level, but no task explicitly drives future-scope discussions with the client. This is appropriate — the methodology is focused on winning the current procurement.

**Implication:** No change needed. This question is measuring something real (relationship depth and client commitment) that the methodology intentionally does not prescribe.

### 5. SS-2: Competitor Relationships (neutralising competitor contacts)
**Gap severity: Moderate**

The Qualify question asks whether the team has identified which client contacts competitors rely on and is executing a neutralisation plan. SAL-10 maps stakeholder disposition (which can include "who supports our competitors") and SAL-03 profiles competitors. But no SAL task explicitly requires mapping competitor-stakeholder relationships and developing neutralisation tactics. The methodology addresses "our relationships" and "our competitors" as separate activities but does not cross-reference them into a competitor relationship neutralisation plan.

**Implication:** The Qualify question probes an important competitive dimension that falls between SAL-03 and SAL-10. The methodology would benefit from an explicit task or quality criterion in SAL-10 that requires assessing competitor stakeholder relationships.

## C. Gap Analysis — SAL Activities NOT Covered by Qualify

These are significant SAL activities, outputs, or intelligence dimensions that represent important qualification intelligence but have no corresponding Qualify question.

### 1. SAL-02: Incumbent Performance Review — ENTIRE ACTIVITY
**Gap severity: Critical**

The SAL methodology devotes an entire activity (SAL-02, 5 effort-days, 10 L3 tasks across 3 L2 sub-processes) to incumbent assessment: service delivery performance, relationship quality, innovation record, price competitiveness, transition stickiness, and transformational disruption potential. This is one of the most important determinants of win probability in government re-competes (which are the majority of opportunities).

**No Qualify question directly asks about the incumbent.** CP-2 (Competitive Intelligence) treats the incumbent as one of several competitors. There is no question that specifically assesses:
- How well we understand the incumbent's performance
- Whether the incumbent is vulnerable or deeply entrenched
- Whether transition stickiness makes displacement realistic
- Whether we have identified transformational opportunities to leapfrog the incumbent
- Whether the evaluation framework will reward disruption

**Implication:** This is the single largest gap in the Qualify question set. Incumbent assessment is arguably the most important qualification dimension for government services bids. A pursuit team could score well on Qualify without ever having assessed whether the incumbent can realistically be unseated.

### 2. SAL-05: Evaluation Criteria Mapping & Scoring Strategy
**Gap severity: Significant**

PI-2 asks whether the team has obtained decision criteria and drivers, which partially maps to SAL-05.1.1. But the scoring strategy dimension — marks concentration, score gap analysis, per-section strategy, win theme alignment to evaluation sections — has no Qualify representation. Understanding the evaluation framework is different from having a strategy to maximise the score within it.

Specifically unaddressed:
- Do we know where the marks are concentrated?
- Can we realistically score maximum marks in every section, or do we have gaps?
- Have we identified scoring gaps and developed mitigations?
- Is our win theme integration aligned to where the marks are?

**Implication:** A pursuit team could know the criteria (PI-2 = Strongly Agree) but have no scoring strategy and no awareness of critical scoring gaps. Adding a question on scoring readiness or gap awareness would capture this.

### 3. SAL-06: Capture Plan Finalisation — Consortium/Partnership Strategy
**Gap severity: Moderate**

SAL-06.3 dedicates three L3 tasks to consortium strategy: structure decision, partner capability requirements, and delivery model work breakdown. No Qualify question assesses whether the team has a partnership/consortium strategy, whether required partner capabilities have been identified, or whether the consortium model is a competitive advantage or a necessary gap-fill.

**Implication:** For complex bids requiring consortium or teaming arrangements, the absence of a partnership/teaming readiness question means Qualify cannot flag this risk dimension.

### 4. SAL-07: Clarification Strategy
**Gap severity: Low**

SAL-07 is a sophisticated activity (using clarifications as a competitive weapon), but it sits downstream of the qualification decision. It would be unusual for a Qualify assessment (which happens at or before bid/no-bid) to assess clarification readiness. This gap is appropriate.

### 5. SAL-01.2: Buyer Values & Client Problem Analysis
**Gap severity: Moderate**

VP-1 asks whether the proposition was co-developed with the client, which indirectly tests buyer value understanding. But no Qualify question directly asks:
- Do we understand what the client strategically cares about (distinct from evaluation criteria)?
- Do we understand the underlying problem driving this procurement?
- Have we distinguished between buyer values and scoring criteria?

SAL-01.2 devotes 3 tasks to this, producing a buyer values register and a client problem statement. The Qualify question set assumes buyer value understanding is embedded in the VP questions, but the VP questions are about our proposition, not about our understanding of what the client values.

**Implication:** A team could have a strong proposition (VP category) without having deep buyer value understanding — they could be lucky rather than informed. A question on buyer value clarity would bridge this gap.

### 6. SAL-06.2: Full Pursuit Qualification & Strategic Fit
**Gap severity: Moderate**

SAL-06.2.2 asks whether this is the right opportunity for the organisation — strategic fit against sector portfolio and growth plan, resource opportunity cost, pipeline balance, reputational risk. No Qualify question assesses organisational strategic fit or resource commitment readiness.

**Implication:** Qualify focuses entirely on "can we win?" and does not ask "should we bid even if we could win?". This is a deliberate scope choice (Qualify is about win probability, not organisational portfolio management), but it means Qualify can produce a high PWIN for a bid the organisation should not pursue.

## D. Framing Assessment — Where Mapped Questions Diverge from SAL Quality Standards

For questions that do map to SAL activities, the following flags where the Qualify question framing (text and rubric) diverges from the SAL methodology's quality criteria.

### D1. CP-1 (Strategy Foundation) vs SAL-06.2.4 (Win Strategy)
**Divergence: Question is broader than the SAL task**

The Qualify question combines route-to-market strategy, competitive approach, team sharing, proven strengths, and competitor gap targeting into a single statement. SAL-06.2.4 synthesises the win strategy narrative but route-to-market is a SAL-06.3 decision (consortium/partnership) and competitor gaps come from SAL-03. The rubric requires "named route-to-market strategy documented and distributed" at Strongly Agree, which aligns well with the methodology's insistence on locked, documented, team-briefed strategy.

**Assessment: Good alignment overall.** The breadth is appropriate for a qualification question — it is a summary test of strategic readiness.

### D2. CP-3 (Winning Proposition) vs SAL-04 (Win Themes)
**Divergence: Question tests stakeholder resonance; SAL-04 tests internal development rigour**

The Qualify question asks whether the proposition "has resonated with key stakeholders and is shaping the client's evaluation criteria." SAL-04's quality criteria focus on whether themes are tested against buyer values, competitively differentiated, evidence-backed, and ITT-aligned. SAL-04 does not include a quality criterion for client validation of themes — that is an SAL-10 engagement outcome.

The Qualify question also asserts "only we can deliver it convincingly" which maps to SAL-04.1.3 (competitive differentiation) but goes further by requiring proof of uniqueness, not just differentiation.

**Assessment: The Qualify question sets a higher bar than the methodology.** SAL-04 can be completed without any client feedback on themes. The Qualify question requires client resonance evidence. This is a deliberate design choice — Qualify is testing capture effectiveness, not methodology compliance.

### D3. PI-2 (Decision Criteria & Drivers) vs SAL-05.1 (Evaluation Framework Analysis)
**Divergence: Different scope — Qualify tests understanding; SAL-05 tests exploitation**

The Qualify rubric at Strongly Agree requires "formal award criteria obtained, priority weighting known, business drivers understood and documented, superiority evidenced or influence plan in place." SAL-05 goes much further: deconstructing the scoring model, mapping criteria to response sections, calculating marks-per-page ratios, and developing per-section scoring strategy.

**Assessment: The Qualify question is pitched at the right level for qualification.** At bid/no-bid, understanding criteria and drivers is the relevant test. The scoring strategy work (SAL-05.2) happens after the qualification decision. However, the Qualify rubric's mention of "demonstrate clear superiority against them" arguably requires scoring strategy awareness that may not exist at qualification stage.

### D4. SS-1 (Internal Champion) vs SAL-10.1.2/10.2.1 (Stakeholder Engagement)
**Divergence: Question demands more than the methodology prescribes**

The Qualify question requires "committed to a joint action plan with specific milestones" and "champion has taken visible action." SAL-10.1.2 assesses disposition (including "champion" as a category) but its quality criteria require only that disposition be assessed and evidence basis cited. SAL-10.2.1 develops engagement plans but does not require joint action plans with client stakeholders.

**Assessment: The Qualify question sets a meaningfully higher bar.** Having a champion is SAL-10 methodology compliance; having a champion with a joint action plan and visible action is capture excellence. The gap is intentional — Qualify should test ambition, not just compliance.

### D5. OI-2 (Personal Agendas) vs SAL-10.1.2 (Stakeholder Assessment)
**Divergence: Qualify probes individual motivation; SAL-10 assesses role-based priorities**

The Qualify question asks how key individuals benefit "personally or professionally" from selecting us. SAL-10.1.2 quality criteria require "known priorities and hot-button issues documented per stakeholder" but frame this as role-based (their professional responsibilities), not personal career ambition or political positioning.

**Assessment: The Qualify question probes a dimension the methodology avoids.** Understanding personal motivations is important but sensitive. The methodology is appropriately neutral; the Qualify question is appropriately challenging.

### D6. VP-1 (Co-Developed) vs SAL-01.2/SAL-04 (Buyer Values/Win Themes)
**Divergence: Question assumes client collaboration; methodology assumes intelligence gathering**

The Qualify question asks whether the value proposition was "shaped through direct collaboration with the client." SAL-01.2 gathers buyer values from intelligence sources (not necessarily from direct client input) and SAL-04 develops win themes internally using that intelligence. The methodology's quality criteria do not require client co-development of the proposition — they require that themes are tested against buyer values.

**Assessment: Significant framing divergence.** The Qualify question implies a pre-ITT engagement model where clients collaborate on proposition development. The methodology is designed to work whether or not there has been pre-ITT engagement. For opportunities with limited pre-ITT access (e.g. framework call-offs, competitive dialogue late entry), the Qualify question's framing penalises teams who are doing the right things within a constrained procurement.

### D7. PP-1 (Client Engagement) vs SAL-10.2.2 (Engagement Execution)
**Divergence: Question tests client investment; methodology tests our engagement execution**

The Qualify question asks whether "the client is investing meaningful time and resource in our collaboration." SAL-10.2.2 logs our engagement activities and outcomes but does not include a quality criterion for client reciprocity or investment. The methodology tracks what we do; the Qualify question tests what the client does in response.

**Assessment: Appropriate divergence.** The client's investment level is a signal, not something the methodology can prescribe. Qualify correctly uses it as a diagnostic.

## E. Recommendations

### E1. ADD a new question: Incumbent Assessment (Category: CP or new)
**Priority: High**

The single largest gap is the absence of any question assessing incumbent understanding. Recommended question:

> "We have assessed the incumbent's delivery performance, relationship strength, and transition stickiness, and have identified specific opportunities to leapfrog their position through transformational approaches that the evaluation framework will reward."

Rubric anchors:
- **Strongly Agree:** Incumbent performance evidenced from multiple sources. Stickiness rated with rationale. Transformational opportunities identified and tested against evaluation framework. Capability to deliver disruption confirmed.
- **Strongly Disagree:** No analysis of incumbent performance or stickiness.

This maps directly to SAL-02 (all three L2 sub-processes). Place in CP category (replacing Countertactics as lowest weight, or adding as a 5th question) or consider a new "Competitive Readiness" sub-dimension.

### E2. ADD a new question: Scoring Gap Awareness (Category: PI)
**Priority: Medium**

Recommended question:

> "We have identified the evaluation sections where most marks are concentrated, assessed our ability to score maximum marks in each, and have specific mitigations for any scoring gaps."

This maps to SAL-05.2 (scoring optimisation strategy). Place in PI category. The current PI questions cover process understanding (approval, criteria, timeline, veto) but not scoring strategy readiness.

### E3. REFRAME VP-1 (Co-Developed) to accommodate constrained procurement
**Priority: Medium**

Current framing: "Our value proposition has been shaped through direct collaboration with the client..."

This penalises teams in constrained procurements (framework call-offs, late-stage competitive dialogue) where direct proposition co-development is not possible. Suggested reframe:

> "Our value proposition is grounded in deep understanding of the client's specific priorities, constraints, and expectations — whether built through direct collaboration, intelligence gathering, or both."

This aligns with SAL-01.2 (buyer values from intelligence) and SAL-04 (win themes from multiple sources) without assuming pre-ITT client access.

### E4. CONSIDER adding: Partnership/Consortium Readiness (Category: CP)
**Priority: Low-Medium**

For complex bids, consortium strategy is a significant qualification dimension. A question could assess:

> "Where this bid requires consortium or partnership capability, we have defined our teaming strategy, identified the right partners, and the consortium model is designed as a competitive advantage."

Maps to SAL-06.3. Lower priority because not all bids require partnerships, and the question would score artificially high for sole-bidder opportunities.

### E5. CONSIDER adding: Buyer Value Understanding (Category: VP)
**Priority: Low-Medium**

The VP questions test our proposition but not our understanding of what the client values. A question like:

> "We can articulate why this procurement exists, what problem the client is solving, and what they strategically care about — distinct from what the evaluation criteria formally measure."

Maps to SAL-01.2.1 (buyer values register) and SAL-01.2.2 (client problem statement). This would distinguish teams who understand the client from teams who merely have a proposition.

### E6. ADJUST weight on CP-4 (Countertactics) — consider reallocating
**Priority: Low**

CP-4 currently has the lowest weight in its category (qw: 0.1). If an incumbent assessment question (E1) is added to CP, consider whether countertactics merits its own question or could be folded into CP-2 (Competitive Intelligence). The rubric for CP-2 already references "tactical counter-plan" — CP-4 adds granularity but little additional signal.

### E7. ADJUST PI-4 (Final Veto Rights) rubric
**Priority: Low**

The rubric asks the team to identify veto holders "at each level of the client's hierarchy," which is very specific. The SAL methodology does not prescribe this level of analysis — SAL-10 identifies DMU members and their influence, but veto risk is not a separate assessment. Consider softening the rubric's Strongly Agree to require identification of veto risk at the most senior level, aligned with what the methodology can realistically produce.

## Conclusion

The PWIN Qualify question set is well-designed for its purpose — it tests capture readiness and forces evidence-based self-assessment. The 24 questions cover the key dimensions of competitive positioning, stakeholder relationships, organisational influence, value proposition, procurement intelligence, and pursuit momentum.

However, the alignment analysis reveals one critical gap: **incumbent assessment is entirely absent from the qualification framework**. Given that the majority of government services opportunities are re-competes where incumbent stickiness is the dominant variable, this is the single most impactful addition that could be made.

The secondary gaps (scoring strategy awareness, buyer value understanding, consortium readiness) are meaningful but less urgent. The framing divergences identified in Section D are mostly intentional design choices — Qualify deliberately sets a higher bar than methodology compliance, which is correct for a capture coaching tool.

The Qualify questions and the SAL methodology are complementary rather than duplicative. Qualify asks "where are you?" in capture readiness; SAL prescribes "what should you do?" to get there. The strongest alignment exists where Qualify questions directly test SAL output quality (CP-2/SAL-03, OI-1/SAL-10, PI-2/SAL-05). The weakest alignment exists where Qualify tests outcomes that no methodology task can prescribe (PP-3 message adoption, PP-4 future focus, VP-2 cost-benefit validation) — and those gaps are appropriate.
